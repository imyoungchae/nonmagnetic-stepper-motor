Automated Measurement of Magnetic Field Uniformity in Helmholtz Coils Using a Nonmagnetic Stepper Motor

Young-Chae Son Sung-Jung Joo Po-Gyu Park Changjin Yun Soo-Chul Lim Hyung-Kew Lee

Abstract

We propose an automated magnetic field uniformity measurement system for Helmholtz coils, leveraging a custom-built nonmagnetic pneumatic stepper motor. To eliminate magnetic interference caused by conventional electric actuators, a fully nonmagnetic motor fabricated from 3D-printed ABS/PLA components and commercial syringes was developed and integrated into a three-axis scanning stage.

The automated system scanned 125 positions within a 4.8 × 4.8 × 4.8 cm³ volume, reducing total measurement time from 90 minutes to 50 minutes and operator involvement from 90 minutes to just 10 minutes. Measurement uncertainty improved from ±0.11% to ±0.06%, and the measured field uniformity of ±0.43% agreed with manual results within the stated uncertainty.

Background

Helmholtz coils are essential instruments for generating highly uniform magnetic fields used in the calibration of magnetic sensors. Evaluating the field uniformity inside a coil requires scanning a Hall sensor across a three-dimensional measurement volume.

The core challenge: Conventional electric actuators generate tens to hundreds of microtesla of stray magnetic field, directly corrupting precision measurements inside Helmholtz coils.

Before this work, technicians at KRISS had to physically enter the measurement space and rotate a mechanical handle to perform the scan manually—a process that took more than 90 minutes per session and was susceptible to human error and fatigue.

System Design

To eliminate magnetic interference at its source, we developed a pneumatic stepper motor driven entirely by compressed air—producing zero electromagnetic field during operation.

Materials (100% nonmagnetic):

3D-printed ABS & PLA structural parts · Plastic bearings · Commercial syringes repurposed as pneumatic cylinders (pistons)

Operating principle: Four pistons apply air pressure sequentially to a gear shaft using a Lock → Push → Release cycle, enabling bidirectional rotation with precise angular control. The motor was mounted directly onto KRISS's existing three-axis scanning stage to provide accurate positioning of the Hall sensor.

System control is executed remotely via pneumatic pressure regulators, keeping all electronics and their associated magnetic fields at a safe distance from the measurement volume.

Experiment & Measurement

The automated stage scanned the interior of a Helmholtz coil across a 4.8 × 4.8 × 4.8 cm³ cubic volume. The Hall sensor was stepped at 1.2 cm intervals along each axis, yielding a uniform grid of 125 measurement points (5 × 5 × 5).

Remote pneumatic control ensured that no part of the electronic control system was near the Helmholtz coil during data acquisition, completely excluding controller-induced magnetic interference from the measurement. To leverage the improved repeatability of the automated system, the number of Hall sensor readings per point was increased from 5 (manual) to 20, directly improving statistical measurement uncertainty.

Conclusion

The nonmagnetic pneumatic stepper motor and automated scanning system developed in this work enable precision magnetic field measurements without any electromagnetic disturbance. By replacing manual operation with a fully automated, remotely controlled stage, we simultaneously:

  • Reduced total measurement time by ~44% (90 min → 50 min)
  • Cut mandatory operator time by more than 89% (90 min → 10 min)
  • Improved measurement uncertainty by nearly half (±0.11% → ±0.06%)
  • Eliminated human error and operator fatigue from the calibration workflow
  • Validated system integrity: automated results agree with manual reference within uncertainty

This technology is directly applicable to Helmholtz coil calibration services and can be extended to other precision magnetic field measurement scenarios where electromagnetic interference from actuators must be avoided.
